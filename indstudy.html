"Anita Bryant Sucks Oranges"

Fusion 360, Maya, Illustrator

In a collaborative research studio hosted by NYU’s Museum Studies department, I worked with graduate and undergraduate students to design an interactive accessibility prototype inspired by Georgia Tech’s Tactile Button Archive. Our goal was to build a multisensory experience for blind and low-vision museum visitors, combining physical design, audio feedback, and RFID/NFC technology.

Ideation

Prior to the end of April, the entire collaboration focused on understanding and learning more about accessible practices in local museums, what practices have worked, what practices have not worked, and what mediums have been the most successful in bridging the gap between being able to attend and enjoy a museum visit while also needing accomodations. In the weeks leading up to April, our team explored gaps in museum accessibility — particularly how non-visual users engage with exhibition content. We drew inspiration from Georgia Tech’s button archive, where pinback buttons triggered audio narratives via RFID readers. We proposed a similar experience: users would scan a tactile button with an embedded NFC chip to trigger context-specific audio on a host device.

Resesarch/Implementation

We consulted with accessibility researchers, 3D modeling specialists, and NYU’s Ability Project to ensure inclusive design practices. Our goals included:

  • Designing tactile buttons with raised lettering and Braille.
  • Prototyping NFC-based audio-trigger systems.
  • Exploring portable RFID interactions to encourage autonomy.

To prepare a prototype of our vision we followed the following steps:

  • Modeled a raised-letter button in Autodesk Maya and refining it in Fusion 360.
  • Conduct user testing with 10 participants to asssess readability of the lettering.
  • Develop a program to trigger audio playback upon NFC chip scan on an RFID reader and iPhone.

Another aspect of the prototype was to build a special closure for the RFID reader to live in, to allow the experience to be more fun and engaging. Considering the history of Anita Bryant, we decided to design a pie shaped enclosure. Originally, I created a 3D model that would be printed and then have the RFID reader live in it, but due to print time as well as the time constraint we had to produce this project, I moved onto laser cutting wood and creating a pie-shaped wood encasing. The wood also additionally failed due to the laser cutter strength not suiting the needs of the cut, limited material, and time. When we presented the prototyped, we showcased the designs of the encasings, but we were not able to physically have them with us.
